Soybeans lead grains higher on fund rotation while livestock marks time ahead of crop data.

🌱BEANS LEAD GRAINS HIGHERMEDIUM CONVICTION
📡DRIVERStrait of Hormuz deal easing input cost pressures, fund rotation into beans
↺Beans: took the fund rotation story that corn was supposed to own.
Soybeans gained 19 cents to $11.32, the biggest single-day move in three weeks as managed money rotated back into the complex. The November contract held steady at $11.32, keeping the carry structure intact while the front month caught up. Corn tagged along for a 3-cent gain to $4.13, but wheat gave back 5 cents to $5.84 on continued fund liquidation. Beans are acting like the funds finally found conviction, not just covering shorts.
Fund money flowing into beans, corn riding coattails, wheat still out of favor.
🐄CATTLE DRIFTS, FEEDERS TRACKLOW CONVICTION
Live cattle eased 0.6% to $241.18, marking time just above the $241 level that's been the pivot since early June. Feeders fell the same percentage to $357.42, keeping the ratio tight at 1.47, the narrowest spread in two months. No fresh news drove the drift; looks like position adjustment ahead of Tuesday's data calendar. The cattle complex is acting patient, not stepped out, but it needs a catalyst to break out of this 10-day range.
Cattle holding pattern above key support, waiting for next catalyst.
🌾OATS FALL ON OVERNIGHT SELLINGMEDIUM CONVICTION
Oats tumbled 11¾ cents to $3.06, a 3.7% single-session decline that wiped out last week's gains. The move started in overnight trade with no specific catalyst, looks like fund liquidation in a thin market. Oats have been the odd grain out all month, lacking the weather premium that's supported corn and beans. At $3.06, the contract is testing support that held in May, but the chart damage is real.
Oats breaking lower on fund selling, thinnest grain getting hit hardest.
⛽ENERGY EASES ON HORMUZ PROGRESSMEDIUM CONVICTION
📡DRIVERFirst LNG tanker clears Hormuz after U.S.-Iran agreement announcement
Crude fell 1.6% to $84.88 as the first LNG tanker cleared Hormuz since the U.S.-Iran deal was announced. Natural gas bucked the trend, up 1.6% to $3.12, but the Hormuz premium that built since early April is deflating fast. The deal doesn't mean swift return to normal flows, but it removes the tail risk that had crude carrying a $8-12 geopolitical premium. Energy input costs for farmers are heading lower.
Hormuz deal deflating energy premium, input costs easing for farmers.
⇄ THE SPREAD TO WATCH
November soybeans / July soybeans carry
Even money, carry structure intact
November beans holding steady at $11.32 while July ran to the same level keeps the carry trade working. When this spread goes inverse, old crop is truly tight. Right now it's telling you there's still time to move beans, the market isn't panicking about summer weather yet.
📍 BASIS PULSE
Eastern Corn Belt basis firm on ethanol demand pickup
Corn basis east of the Mississippi is firming as ethanol plants return from seasonal maintenance. The River is paying 10-15 cents over for June delivery, best premium since April. Western Belt staying soft, normal for the seasonal.
🧠 THE MORE YOU KNOW
The feeder-live ratio: why 1.47 is the magic number
Today's feeder-to-live cattle ratio hit 1.47, the tightest in two months. When this ratio drops below 1.45, feeders historically outperform live cattle by an average 200 basis points over the next 30 days. The math is simple: tight ratios mean feeders are cheap relative to the end product, usually because feed costs are cooperating or because live cattle demand is stronger than expected. At 1.47, we're one tick away from the historical trigger level that says buy feeders, sell live cattle.
